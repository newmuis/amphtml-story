Star power:
NASA discovers 7 Earth-size planets

It's the first time astronomers have detected so many terrestrial planets orbiting a single star. And they could harbor life.

February 22

A newfound solar system just 39 light-years away contains seven warm, rocky planets, NASA announced Wednesday

An artist's conception of the view from the surface of the exoplanet TRAPPIST-1f. (Illustration by NASA/JPL-Caltech)

The newly discovered solar system resembles a scaled-down version of our own

The star at its center, an “ultra-cool dwarf,” is less than a tenth the size of our sun and about a quarter as warm. Its planets circle tightly around it; the closest takes just a day and a half to complete an orbit and the most distant takes about 20 days.

Seven Earth-sized planets transit across the face of a dim, dwarf star called TRAPPIST-1

Scientists have been interested in TRAPPIST-1 since 2015. Using the Transiting Planets and Planetesimals Small Telescope (TRAPPIST), they sensed small dips in the star's brightness at regular intervals. These dips were caused by 3 planets transiting — crossing between the star and Earth — and blocking some of its light.

Instead of three planets, TRAPPIST had seven, renamed TRAPPIST-1b through -h in order of their distance from the star.

Three of the planets of the TRAPPIST-1 system are in the “habitable zone.”
